Welcome!

The Ferdowsi Research Laboratory uses a combination of laboratory and numerical experiments, as well as theoretical and analytical studies, to better understand and more quantitatively predict the flow and deformation (that is the scientific field of Rheology) of Earth materials in (initial and transient loading, boundary) conditions relevant to Earth's near-surface processes. A major focus of the group currently is on the behavior of granular materials -- as well as other complex fluids and disordered solids that share some similarity in their structural properties and dynamical behavior with granular materials -- in dry, fluid-driven, saturated, and unsaturated conditions. For numerical methods, we use and collaborate to further develop, Discrete Element Method (DEM), DEM coupled to Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) method, DEM coupled to Finite Element Method (FEM) method, and Boundary Element Method (BEM). For laboratory experiments, we collaborate with classical rock and sediment friction and mechanics laboratories; we are also equipped with an advanced rheometery laboratory capable of performing rheology experiments on soft geometerials, granular materials, and complex fluids, in conditions relevant to Earth's near-surface. We also seek collaborations with field-going scientists, and more broadly across the disciplinary lines with geophysicists, geologists, engineers, applied mathematicians, computational scientists, social scientists, environmental policy researchers and scientists.

In addition to the advanced rheometer (an Anton Paar Modular Compact Rheometer MCR 502e WESP, with fluid pressure cell, temperature control cell, several loading geometries, imaging and particle tracking setup, accelorometers), the lab is also equipped with a dedicated computational cluster (with ~800 CPU cores) maintained by the Hewlett Packard Enterprise Data Science Institute (HPE DSI) at the University of Houston.

So, why do we study flow and deformation of Earth materials and granular materials? I (Behrooz) has a few primary reasons that I would be happy to explain here, and as the laboratory grows, these motivations will be updated based on the interests and with collaboration of other members of the lab. Behrooz's first reason has been curiosity, I have been intensely curious about various aspects of the behavior of Earth materials that are granular or those that have a disordered and granular-like structure.


My second reason is kind of derivative of this first reason: we still know very little about various important aspects of the behavior of granular geomaterials (note that the Noble Prize in Physics 2021 has been awarded in part to Giorgio Parisi for his scientific discoveries related to advancing our fundamental understanding of the behavior of disordered solids, including granular materials), and what my research and research of many others in our field shows, is that an improved understanding of the behavior of granular materials, is crucial for developing physics-based (accurate and with an understanding of why a certain behavior emerges, or certain equations or components are included) models for flow and deformation of a broad range of earth materials. In particular, my most recent research (with Allan M. Rubin, my former postdoctoral advisor in the Department of Geosciences at Princeton University) shows that a transient frictional response that many geomaterials in localized shear zones show (known as the rate- and state-dependent friction) can be a granular physics phenomenon. This transient frictional response is key to the initiation of almost all geological instabilities and hazards with a frictional origin, including landslides and earthflows, sliding of glaciers and nucleation of icequakes, earthquake nucleation, volcanic caldera collapse); we currently have no other reliable physics-based model or phenomenology that can explain the rate- and state-dependent friction as seen in the lab, at least for rock shear zones), so improving our predictive capabilities of these variety of geological hazards and processes that change (and/or are changing in response to natural forcings, climate change, anthropogenic forcing) the Earth's surface and the environment became a new emerging motivation for my research.

As a third reason, I am also broadly interested in understanding the physics and chemistry of the processes that shape and change the Earth's surface over short (~human life) and long (geological) timescales. These include the physics of sediment transport in rivers and streams, and physics of soil transport and soil production, both of which are important phenomena for forecasting changes of the Earth's surface over short and long timescales; soil and sediment transport topics are also not too distant from granular physics (soils at some scale are made of grains; sediment transport in rivers or fluvial setting is a hydrogranular process). This description is to be completed with links and information to my past and ongoing research in the aforementioned research topics. For now, you can check my publication page, or reach out to me directly via email, if you are interested in discussing about any of these areas for research or for potential collaboration.
