Welcome!

The Ferdowsi Research Laboratory uses a combination of laboratory and numerical experiments, as well as theoretical and analytical studies, to better understand and more quantitatively predict the flow and deformation behavior (that is the scientific field of rheology) of Earth materials in (initial and transient loading, boundary) conditions relevant to Earth's near-surface processes. A major focus of the group currently is on the behavior of granular materials -- and other complex fluids and disordered solids that share some similarity in their structural properties and dynamical behavior with granular materials -- in dry, fluid-driven, saturated, and unsaturated conditions. For numerical experiments, we use and collaborate to further develop, Discrete Element Method (DEM), DEM coupled to Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) method, DEM coupled to Finite Element Method (FEM) method, and Boundary Element Method (BEM). For laboratory experiments, we collaborate with classical rock and sediment friction and mechanics laboratories; we are also equipped with an advanced rheometery laboratory which is capable of performing (steady-state and transient) rheology experiments on soft geometerials, granular materials, and complex fluids, in conditions relevant to Earth's near-surface. We seek collaborations with field-going scientists, and are interested in collaborations across the disciplinary lines with geophysicists, geologists, engineers, applied mathematicians, computational scientists, social scientists, environmental policy researchers and scientists.

The lab is currently equipped with an advanced rheometer (an Anton Paar Modular Compact MCR 502e WESP, with fluid pressure cell, temperature control cell, several loading geometries, imaging and particle tracking setup, accelorometers). We also have a dedicated computational cluster (with ~800 CPU cores) for running numerical experiments, which is maintained by the Hewlett Packard Enterprise Data Science Institute (HPE DSI) at the University of Houston.

So, why do we study flow and deformation of Earth materials and granular materials? I (Behrooz) has a few main reasons that I will explain here. As the laboratory grows, these motivations will be updated based on the interests and with collaboration of other members of the lab. For the first reason, I have been intensely curious1 about various aspects of the behavior of Earth materials that are granular or those that have a disordered and granular-like structure. My second reason is derived from this first reason: we still know very little about various important aspects (structural and dynamical) of the behavior of granular geomaterials2. My research and research of other researchers in our field show that an improved understanding of the behavior of granular materials is crucial for developing physics-based models3 for broad categories of earth materials. Specifically and as an example, my most recent research (with Allan M. Rubin, my former postdoctoral advisor in the Department of Geosciences at Princeton University) shows that a transient frictional response that many geomaterials show (known as the rate- and state-dependent friction) can be a granular physics phenomenon. This transient frictional response is key to the initiation of almost all geological instabilities and natural hazards with a frictional origin, including landslides and earthflows, sliding of glaciers and nucleation of icequakes, earthquake nucleation, volcanic caldera collapse; we also currently have no other physics-based model or phenomenology that can explain the transient (rate- and state-dependent frictional) response, as seen in the lab, at least for rock shear zones. So, improving our predictive capabilities for frictional geological hazards and processes becomes an emerging motivation for this research.
In addition to the aforementioned motivations, we are also broadly interested in understanding the physics and chemistry of the processes that shape and change the Earth's surface over short (~human life) and long (geological) timescales. These include the physics (or physical-chemistry) of sediment transport in rivers and streams, and soil transport and soil production, both of which are important phenomena for forecasting changes of the Earth's surface. Soil and sediment transport phenomena are also not too distant from granular physics, because most soils at some scale are made of grains; sediment transport in rivers or fluvial settings is a hydrogranular process.
